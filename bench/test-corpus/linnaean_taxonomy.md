# The Linnaean Taxonomy

Carl Linnaeus, an 18th-century Swedish naturalist, devised the
hierarchical classification system that biology still uses, with
modifications, today. His landmark work *Systema Naturae* first
appeared in 1735 as a 12-page pamphlet. By its tenth edition in 1758
it had grown to two volumes and codified the **binomial
nomenclature** that gives every species a two-part Latin name like
*Homo sapiens* or *Quercus alba*.

## The ranks

Linnaeus's original three "kingdoms" were Animalia, Plantae, and
Mineralia — the third has been retired as not biological. Modern
biology uses these ranks, in nesting order from broadest to
narrowest:

1. **Domain** — Bacteria, Archaea, or Eukarya.
2. **Kingdom** — within Eukarya: Animalia, Plantae, Fungi, Protista.
3. **Phylum** — within Animalia, e.g. Chordata (animals with a
   notochord), Arthropoda (insects, crustaceans, spiders).
4. **Class** — Mammalia, Reptilia, Aves, Insecta, Arachnida.
5. **Order** — Carnivora, Primates, Cetacea, Coleoptera (beetles).
6. **Family** — Felidae (cats), Canidae (dogs), Hominidae (great apes).
7. **Genus** — *Panthera*, *Canis*, *Homo*, *Quercus*.
8. **Species** — *Panthera leo*, *Canis lupus*, *Homo sapiens*.

The mnemonic "Dear King Phillip Came Over For Good Soup" gets you
through them in order.

## Binomial nomenclature

Each species is identified by its genus + species epithet, written
in italic with the genus capitalized. Subspecies append a third
term, typically not italicized: *Felis silvestris catus* (the
domestic cat) is the subspecies of the European wildcat.

The naming authority — the person who first formally described the
species — is sometimes appended in parentheses: *Homo sapiens*
Linnaeus, 1758. The 1758 publication date is the formal cutoff for
priority claims under the International Code of Zoological
Nomenclature.

## Key examples

- **Tigers:** *Panthera tigris*. The Bengal tiger is the subspecies
  *Panthera tigris tigris*; the Siberian is *Panthera tigris
  altaica*. Recent genetic work argues for collapsing all subspecies
  into just two (continental and Sunda).
- **Dogs vs wolves:** *Canis lupus familiaris* (dog) is the
  subspecies of *Canis lupus* (gray wolf). They can interbreed
  freely, which by the biological species concept makes them the
  same species — a fact occasionally controversial among breeders
  but settled among biologists.
- **Mushrooms:** the kingdom Fungi was elevated from being lumped
  under Plantae in the mid-20th century. Mushrooms are more closely
  related to animals than to plants — both descended from a single-
  celled ancestor that gave rise to the opisthokont lineage.

## The clades that broke Linnaeus

Modern phylogenetic systematics, building on the work of Willi
Hennig in the 1950s, supplements the rank system with **clades** —
groups defined by descent from a common ancestor rather than by
shared traits. Clade-based taxonomy reveals that some Linnaean
classes are paraphyletic: **Reptilia** as traditionally defined
excludes birds, but birds are nested inside the reptile family
tree, so a "reptile" group that excludes them is artificial.
Modern usage either expands Reptilia to include birds or replaces
it with the clade **Sauropsida**.

## Why this still matters

Despite the philosophical shift toward clades, the Linnaean ranks
persist because they're what museum collections, field guides, and
working biologists are trained on. The system is also legally
codified — environmental protection laws reference species, not
clades, and changing a species' name has cascading consequences
for endangered-species lists.
